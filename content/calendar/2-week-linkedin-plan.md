### Tuesday, Feb 25 ‚Äî Playbook Post (Resume)

**Pillar:** Playbook | **Source:** #35, #262 (Resume as Checklist + F-Pattern Reading)

---

Stop writing your resume like a biography.

I see this ALL the time. People writing paragraph after paragraph about their career history. What they did. Where they worked. Blah blah blah.

Here's what hiring managers actually do:

They scan your resume in an F-pattern. Top to bottom, left to right. 6 seconds. That's it.

So let's flip the script.

Treat the job posting like a checklist:

‚úÖ Years of experience? Check.
‚úÖ Specific tools they mentioned? Check.
‚úÖ Industry background? Check.
‚úÖ Leadership experience? Check.

Then make sure every single one of those checkboxes is visible in the TOP THIRD of your resume.

Not buried on page 2. Not hidden in a paragraph. Right there. Up front. Where the F-pattern hits.

Your resume isn't your life story. It's an ad. And you've got 6 seconds to make the sale.

One thing you can do today: pull up your resume and the last job posting you applied to. Count how many of their requirements appear in your first 5 bullet points.

If the answer is less than 3, we've got work to do.

### Thursday, Feb 27 ‚Äî Q&A Post (Interviews)

**Pillar:** Q&A | **Source:** #47 (How to Answer Questions About Experience You Don't Have)

---

"What's your experience with [thing I've never done]?"

This question makes people freeze. I get it.

Here's exactly what to say:

"I don't have direct experience with X, but I DO have experience with Y, which is similar because..."

Then you tell them:
‚Üí The challenges you faced
‚Üí The results you got
‚Üí What you learned that transfers directly

That's it. No apologizing. No spiraling. No "I'm sorry, I don't have that."

You redirect.

I coached someone last month who got asked about a specific software platform she'd never touched. She said: "I haven't used that exact tool, but I spent 3 years on a similar platform, and in that time I automated a reporting process that saved my team 10 hours a week."

She got the offer.

Nobody has 100% of the qualifications. NOBODY. Not even the person who wrote the job posting.

Your job isn't to have every answer. It's to show them how you think, how you learn, and what you bring.

Try this: right now, think of one skill you DON'T have that keeps showing up in job postings. Then write down your closest experience. That's how you redirect.

The result? A rate equivalent to **$260,000 per year.**

From $90K to $260K.

Not because she's some negotiation genius. Because she did the math, knew her number, and asked for it.

I want to talk about something nobody talks about.

The fear of the BEST-case scenario.

One of my clients was about to post an "open to work" update on LinkedIn. She froze. Not because she was afraid nobody would respond.

Because she was afraid people WOULD respond.

If they responded, she'd have to do interviews. And her last interview experience was brutal. An interviewer (a senior engineer, 15 years older) grilled her on every gap in her knowledge for 45 minutes straight.

So her brain did that thing brains do: it decided the safest option was to not put herself out there at all.

The fear doesn't go away before you act. It goes away BECAUSE you act.

What's the one thing you've been avoiding because the best-case scenario scares you?

### Tuesday, Mar 4 ‚Äî Playbook Post (Networking)

**Pillar:** Playbook | **Source:** #132, #240 (Pitch Slapping + Give Before You Ask)

---

Stop pitch-slapping people on LinkedIn.

If you don't know the term: pitch slapping is when you connect with someone and IMMEDIATELY ask them for something. A job. A referral. A "quick 15-minute chat."

You wouldn't walk up to a stranger at a party and say "Hi, can you get me a job?" So why do it on LinkedIn?

Here's what to do instead:

**Give before you ask. Every single time.**

5 ways to give value BEFORE you need something:

1Ô∏è‚É£ **Reference their work.** "I saw your talk at [event] and your point about X changed how I think about Y."

2Ô∏è‚É£ **Share their content.** Repost something they wrote. Add your own take. Tag them.

3Ô∏è‚É£ **Send a relevant article.** "Saw this and thought of our conversation about [topic]."

4Ô∏è‚É£ **Make an introduction.** Connect them with someone THEY would benefit from knowing.

5Ô∏è‚É£ **Close the loop.** If they gave you advice before, tell them you used it and what happened.

The people who get replies on LinkedIn aren't the ones who ask the best questions. They're the ones who give the most value first.

Try this today: pick ONE person you want to connect with. Find something they've posted. Leave a thoughtful comment. That's it. No ask. Just value.

The ask comes later. And when it does, they'll actually want to help.

### Saturday, Mar 8 ‚Äî Playbook Post (Interviews)

**Pillar:** Playbook | **Source:** #197, #220 (Four Core Story Archetypes + Recruiter's Prep Framework)

---

A recruiter told me something that blew my mind:

"Most candidates spend ZERO hours preparing for interviews."

Zero. They just show up and wing it.

So here's your cheat code: spend 1-2 hours prepping, and you're already ahead of 90% of candidates.

Here's EXACTLY how to use those 2 hours:

**Prepare 4 stories. That's it. Just 4.**

1Ô∏è‚É£ Your biggest success story (specific project, clear result)
2Ô∏è‚É£ Your biggest failure and what you learned
3Ô∏è‚É£ A time you dealt with a difficult person
4Ô∏è‚É£ A passion project or something that lights you up

Why these 4? Because almost every behavioral interview question is a variation of one of them.

"Tell me about a challenge" = Story #2 or #3
"What are you proud of?" = Story #1
"Why this role?" = Story #4

For each story, write it out using the STAR format:
‚Üí **S**ituation (2 sentences max)
‚Üí **T**ask (what was your specific role?)
‚Üí **A**ction (what did YOU do, step by step?)
‚Üí **R**esult (specific numbers if possible)

Then practice saying them out loud. Not reading them. SAYING them.

The people who ace interviews aren't smoother or smarter. They're more prepared.

Two hours. Four stories. That's the whole game.
